Connecticut Private Investigator Offers Advice on What to do when You Suspect that Your Spouse is Cheating

Leading private investigator says that suspicions do not always lead to proof and there are ways to save a relationship when you feel that something might be wrong.

“Thoughts of an affair, whether real and suspected, are a common hazard of the modern relationship and they can take an enormous toll on both parties,” says Christopher Paoletti, Founder and President of Infidelity Investigations (http://www.infidelitypolice.com), a Connecticut private investigation firm that specializes in infidelity cases. “First, there is the devastating realization that the person you love may be attracted to someone else. Then the question of what may or may not be going on arises, which can leave a person physically, mentally and emotionally depleted.”

Paoletti says that it is important to know that, sometimes, a spouse's affair or flirtation with the idea of an affair may be nothing more than a way to explore some aspect of his or her identity. This is especially true, he says, when the couple married young and their life experiences are limited.

“Often, the victim of a cheating spouse does not want to confront their partner or doesn’t want to know the truth,” says Paoletti. “If this is the case, and the victim just wants to salvage their relationship, there are a number of measures that can be taken.”

Paoletti suggests asking the spouse or partner to set aside a specific amount of time, 45 minutes for example, every day to just be together and talk. Another idea that he suggests is introducing the subject of a friend or neighbor's marital affairs and ask for thoughts on why people are unfaithful to their partners.

“Partners can also ask their spouse what changes he or she would like to see in the relationship,” says Paoletti. “Let your spouse know that you are committed to making your relationship grow and improve and then ask for their commitment as well.”

Paoletti suggests that partners continue to keep an open line of communication.

“Marriages can come back even from the devastating blow of infidelity, as long as both partners are willing to examine themselves and their contributions to the positive growth of their relationship,” says Paoletti. “Periodic evaluation and re-commitment are essential to sustaining any long term relationship.”
